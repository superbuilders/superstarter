        sub_type_id         |    kind     |                                                                                     text
----------------------------+-------------+-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 numerical.averages         | recognition | For 'one element added/removed' questions, the new value's distance from the old mean tells you the shift — you rarely have to recompute the whole average.
 numerical.averages         | technique   | Compute the delta from the mean and redistribute over the new count: (new − old_mean) ÷ new_count = mean_shift. Faster than re-averaging.
 numerical.averages         | trap        | 'Average rate' or 'average speed' is not the arithmetic mean of the rates — use total quantity over total denominator (total distance ÷ total time).
 numerical.fractions        | recognition | Anchor every fraction against 1/2 (or 1) at a glance: 7/13 < 1/2, 14/15 ≈ 1. Most comparisons resolve before any calculation.
 numerical.fractions        | technique   | Cross-multiply for two-fraction comparisons (a/b vs c/d → ad vs bc). For 'highest value' near 1, compare the *remaining* part instead (1/15 < 1/13).
 numerical.fractions        | trap        | Don't reflexively find a common denominator on a 4-option fractions question — that's a 30-second computation when 5 seconds of estimation will do.
 numerical.number_series    | recognition | Test differences between consecutive terms first, then ratios, then second-order differences. Most series resolve at the first level.
 numerical.number_series    | technique   | If the linear test fails, scan for an interleaved pair (positions 1, 3, 5 vs 2, 4, 6) before assuming a complex rule.
 numerical.number_series    | trap        | Don't fall in love with the first pattern that fits two terms — verify it against at least three before committing.
 numerical.percentages      | recognition | Find 10% by shifting the decimal one place left, then scale (×3 for 30%, ×7 for 70%). This handles most percent-of-whole questions in one step.
 numerical.percentages      | technique   | On consecutive percent changes, anchor on the *new* base after each step. 'Up 50% then down 50%' lands at 75% of the original, not 100%.
 numerical.percentages      | trap        | 'X is what percent of Y' and 'Y is what percent of X' have different denominators — confirm which way the question runs before computing.
 numerical.ratios           | recognition | Decide first whether the question asks parts-to-parts (3:2 means 3 cats per 2 dogs) or parts-to-whole (3:2 means 3 of every 5 are cats). The answer keys to that distinction.
 numerical.ratios           | technique   | Scale the ratio to match the question's known quantity. 3:2 with 9 cats → multiply by 3 to get 9:6, so 6 dogs.
 numerical.ratios           | trap        | A ratio of 7:9 is not 'split into 7 and 9'; it's 'split into 16 parts'. Setting up 7x + 9x = total before solving prevents the most common error.
 numerical.word_problems    | recognition | Skim the question for the units it asks for (dollars, hours, miles per hour) before reading the body — it tells you what equation to set up.
 numerical.word_problems    | technique   | Sketch the relationship (timeline, two-circle diagram, or rate × time = distance table) before computing. Translation is the bottleneck, not arithmetic.
 numerical.word_problems    | trap        | When the answer choices are clustered (12, 14, 15, 16), don't trust mental arithmetic — work the problem at least once on paper before picking.
 verbal.analogies           | recognition | Name the relationship between the first pair in plain language ('a bird's primary mode of locomotion is flight') before looking at any option.
 verbal.analogies           | technique   | Substitute the relationship template into each option: '{X} is to {Y} as {first}'s primary {relationship} is to {second}'. Whichever survives is the answer.
 verbal.analogies           | trap        | Don't pick an option just because both words feel related to the target pair — the relationship has to match in kind and direction, not topic.
 verbal.antonyms            | recognition | Read the target word, say its opposite out loud in your head, then pick the option closest to what you said. Don't reverse-engineer from the options.
 verbal.antonyms            | technique   | When two options seem opposite to the target, the correct answer is usually the more general opposite, not the most extreme one.
 verbal.antonyms            | trap        | Words with multiple meanings (e.g., 'late' meaning both tardy and deceased) often have antonyms keyed to the less obvious sense — check every reading before locking in.
 verbal.critical_reasoning  | recognition | Spatial-direction problems and syllogisms reward sketching the relationships ('David west of Katrina; Nathan west of David → N-D-K'). Don't try to hold them in your head.
 verbal.critical_reasoning  | technique   | Treat the premises as a closed world. Anything you 'know' from outside the prompt is irrelevant to the conclusion.
 verbal.critical_reasoning  | trap        | When a conclusion sounds strong, prefer 'Uncertain' unless the premises explicitly support it — the test rewards the most modest defensible answer.
 verbal.letter_series       | recognition | Convert each letter to its alphabet position (A=1, B=2, …) any time the pattern doesn't resolve at a glance — pretend it's a number series.
 verbal.letter_series       | technique   | On multi-letter sequences (xrfm, xqen, xpdo, …), treat each character position as its own independent series and solve them in parallel.
 verbal.letter_series       | trap        | Don't count alphabet positions on your fingers in real time — that's where seconds vanish. Memorize milestones (E=5, J=10, O=15, T=20).
 verbal.sentence_completion | recognition | Conjunctions like 'although', 'because', and 'despite' telegraph whether the missing word agrees or contrasts with the surrounding text — read for those first.
 verbal.sentence_completion | technique   | On double-blank questions, eliminate any option whose first word fails before evaluating the second word.
 verbal.sentence_completion | trap        | An option can be grammatically correct and still wrong — the test wants the choice that makes the sentence cohere, not just parse.
(33 rows)


Discipline extracted (per plan §2(c) and §3 pin table):

- Length:        18–45 words per entry; median ~25. Recognition shortest;
                 technique often longest (worked formula or substitution
                 template); trap moderate.
- Tone:          Imperative second-person primary ("Read the target word",
                 "Don't pick", "Anchor every fraction"); descriptive
                 third-person for tactic explanation ("'Up 50% then down 50%'
                 lands at 75% of the original, not 100%"). No first-person.
                 No hedging ("you might want to consider").
- Structure:     Single tactic-sentence + a parenthetical concrete-example or
                 numeric-anchor (or after an em-dash). The anchor is
                 load-bearing — it's the difference between generic test-prep
                 advice and a sub-type-specific tactic.
- Cadence:       Three orthogonal tactics per sub-type, not three drilldowns
                 of one tactic. Recognition = parse-fast / spot-the-structure
                 tactic; Technique = compute-efficiently tactic; Trap = the
                 most common error or distractor pattern.
- Specificity:   Concrete to the sub-type's problem patterns; references
                 operators, numeric anchors, named pitfalls. Generic test-
                 prep advice ("read carefully", "manage time") never appears.
- Punctuation:   Real Unicode characters: em-dash (—), multiplication (×),
                 division (÷), approximation (≈), set ellipsis (…). Not ASCII
                 placeholders. The post-session surface renders verbatim.
- Ordering sig:  None at the data level (no `ordering` column). `kind` carries
                 semantic role. <StrategySurface> sorts by section + alpha by
                 displayName at render — kind is not a render-sort key.

Three problem-pattern clusters:
  (i)   d = s × t solved-for-the-missing-variable: the train, the car,
        the runner, Jesse-from-departure, Jody-from-arrival.
  (ii)  Unit-conversion (km↔miles, hours↔minutes, seconds↔fractions of

        36-minute window; the rocket uses km per ¼-second over a 7-second
        window. Unit-normalization is the recurring step.
  (iii) Compound-rate (upstream/downstream, with-wind/against-wind): the
        boat downstream at 120 km / 4h with stream 5 km/h, solving for
        still-water speed = surface-rate − stream-rate. This pattern's
        trap: using surface rate where vehicle's still-water rate is
        required.

  Two-mover relative-position problems (Dan + Peter) reduce to (i) on
  rate-difference × time.

            |                                     |  "3/8"]

            |                                     |  "1*0.8", "5*0.2",
            |                                     |  "4*0.5"]

Stem is uniformly "Which number has the lowest value?" or its inverse
"Which number has the highest value?" The comparison universe lives entirely
in options_json. Three problem-pattern clusters:
  (i)   Cross-notation comparison (decimals vs fractions vs arithmetic
        expressions vs fraction-of-quantity): the "0.03 vs 3/10 vs 0.3 vs
        0.2+0.15 vs 2/3" set; "1/8 of 32" vs "2/7 of 21".
  (ii)  Compute-each-option-then-compare: ".3*.5 vs .4-.15 vs .25+.18 vs
        .6-.41" — each option resolves to a small decimal; comparison is
        on the resolved values.
  (iii) Anchor-and-eliminate: use a reference point (½, 1, a known
        integer) to discard candidates without computing each one in full.
        The "0.6 vs 2/3 vs 5/6 vs 0.5 vs 0.674" set rewards anchoring
        against ½ to discard 5/6 and 0.674 immediately.

Direction-flips (lowest vs highest) appear in roughly 1 in 8 items based
on this sample; a misread on direction is the most common trap.

<StrategySurface> (src/components/post-session/strategy-surface.tsx):
- "use client" directive; purely presentational.
- Receives `strategies: ReadonlyArray<SurfacedStrategy>` already pre-selected
  upstream (one entry per struggled sub-type).
- Renders an unordered list. Each row shows a `displayName` prefix in
  font-medium followed by the strategy text.
- Sort key: section (verbal first), then alphabetical by displayName. KIND IS
  NOT A SORT KEY at render time.
- Empty-array branch renders "No sub-types flagged this session — keep going."
- Test-id selectors: data-testid="post-session-strategy-surface-section" on the
  section; data-testid={`post-session-strategy-row-${row.subTypeId}`} per row.

src/server/post-session/strategy-selection.ts — the selection pipeline:

  STRUGGLED_ACCURACY_FLOOR = 0.7
  THRESHOLD_BY_SUB_TYPE = ReadonlyMap<SubTypeId, number>  (from sub-types config)

  type FailureMode =
    | "fast-wrong"     // accuracy < 0.7 AND median latency ≤ threshold
    | "slow-wrong"     // accuracy < 0.7 AND median latency > threshold
    | "slow-but-right" // accuracy ≥ 0.7 AND median latency > threshold
    | "not-struggled"  // accuracy ≥ 0.7 AND median latency ≤ threshold

  preferredKind(mode) → KindPreference | null:
    fast-wrong     → { primary: "trap",        fallback: "technique" }
    slow-wrong     → { primary: "recognition", fallback: "technique" }
    slow-but-right → { primary: "recognition", fallback: "technique" }
    not-struggled  → null

  pickOneStrategy(strategies, mode) → SurfacedStrategy | null:
    1. Look for an entry matching the primary kind.
    2. Fall back to an entry matching the fallback kind.
    3. Last resort: return any strategy that exists for this sub-type.
    4. If the array is empty: return null.

  selectStrategiesForStruggledSubTypes(accuracy, latency, allStrategies):
    - deriveStruggledSubTypes(...) over accuracy+latency.
    - groupStrategiesBySubType(allStrategies).
    - For each struggled sub-type:
        if strategiesForSubType === undefined or .length === 0 → continue
        else surface pickOneStrategy(strategiesForSubType, mode).

LOAD-BEARING IMPLICATION:
The selection logic guarantees that to render a non-fallback (kind-correct)
entry under any failure mode, a sub-type needs ALL THREE KINDS. Without a
`trap` entry, fast-wrong sessions fall through to the `technique` fallback;
without `recognition`, slow-wrong / slow-but-right sessions do the same.
Without all three, the "any" last-resort branch is reachable. The discipline
in §3 — exactly one entry per (sub_type_id, kind) — is what makes
failure-mode → kind preference work without lossy fallbacks. The new 9 entries
authored at commit 2 must satisfy this completeness invariant per sub-type.
